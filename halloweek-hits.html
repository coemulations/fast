Halloweek Hits

(And the misses you want to avoid)

  • October 29, 2019
  • Rebecca Mazzu

The hype for Halloween has begun. We have come a long way from trick or treating days, although I am still a big supporter of this tradition; however, now the term is synonymous with a weekend of parties. As you search for your costume or costumes, we want to help you look your best while at the same time being culturally sensitive.

We talk a lot about cultural appropriation, but it’s important to define this term: it is commonly referred to as the adoption of aspects of one culture by individuals not from said culture. The term itself does not carry a negative meaning; however, the frequent perpetuation of cultural appropriation by historically dominant cultures over minority cultures has given the term a negative connotation. It is important to understand that most anthropologistsargue culture is fluid, and elements of different cultures often due become assimilated into those of others. So, when is cultural appropriation wrong? In basic terms cultural appropriation is frowned upon when it paints a caricaturebased on stereotypes of a group, or when it sexualizes said group.

Here are some of the biggest cultural appropriation misses, so read carefully to make sure your outfit is a hit.

1. Don't wear skin as a costume.

This isn’t even cultural appropriation, it’s racism dating back to the early 20thcentury. Wearing blackface is an absolute no-no. You should not under any circumstances imitate the physical attributes of a group of people. If you dress up as a celebrity or character that is of a race other than yours, as a rule of thumb your costume should be able to express the character you are dressed up as without any physical attributes to indicate race.

2. Don't wear sacred artifacts.

Native American war bonnets, hijabs, among others have a cultural and sacred meaning for members of the cultures they pertain to, and therefore are also important to avoid. How about wearing a cross? Wearing a cross if you are not Christian is cultural appropriation, however it does not fall under the negative connotation of the term as Christians have seldom been persecuted in the western world. However, the cross is a holy symbol and regardless of whether or not the group has been oppressed, sacred artifacts should be avoided. Even if you understand the historical background of the item you want to wear, you do not value it as the members of the cultural group.

3.Don’t wear traditional costume of oppressed groups

The Roma people seem to be constant favorites for Halloween, and at this point you might be asking yourself: the who? Which furthers my point; Roma’s commonly known as gypsies, have been persecuted for centuries and for that reason dressing as a gypsy is not ‘cute’. Emphasis in this section on oppressed people, as long as the costume holds no sacred meaning, usingtraditional costume of another cultural group that hasn’t been oppressed is usually appropriate. For example, wearing a dirndl, that is the traditional Bavarian garment used popularly during Octoberfest festivities, is appropriate, because it is not sacred nor have the German people been oppressed.

4.Don’t wear anything you would have to explain as being a fashion statement

Because it just isn’t. Cornrows among other black hairstyles have been consistently seen as unprofessional or pretty according to Eurocentric beautystandards, so when non-black people use their hairstyles they are gaining attention for something black women have been oppressed for and forced into permanently altering their hair.

While these issues are very pertinent during the Halloween period, we shouldall be aware of them throughout the year, especially festival season.Now, are you feeling a little nervous? Don’t know what to wear? And especially don’t want to offend anyone? We compiled a list of outfits that are appropriate and are cute or sexy, whichever way you swing for Halloween.

Classic Halloween

Scary costumes for Halloween? Ground breaking. Make it cute, make it sexy, either way it’s hard to be culturally offensive when you’re dressed as a skeleton.

Animals

I’d say as long as you don’t wear any items where animals were hurt in the process of making that garment you’re safe with an animal costume. Make a statement: dress up as an endangered species due to climate change like a polar bear.

Non-human Cartoon Characters

Disney, Nickelodeon, and Cartoon Network blessed our generation with amazing characters from iconic Mickey Mouse to my personal favorite My Little Pony.

Occupation

If you’re going for sexy this is always a good option, and they’re perfect for group costumes. Think outside the box: sexy chef, sexy CEO, sexy zookeeper. Disclaimer: while some religious figures fill occupations such as Rabbis and Priests, avoid these costumes, as these are sacred robes.

Our tips aren’t absolute truth, people have different opinions on what’s appropriate, just remember to be conscious of what inspired this costume. We hope our advice gets everyone at your parties to be staring at you for the right reasons! Happy Halloween!
